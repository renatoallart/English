“Eeny meeny miney mo, Catch a tigger (= tiger) by his toe; If he hollers, let him go, Eeny meeny miney mo.”
eeny meeny miny moe /ˌiːni ˌmiːni ˌmaɪni ˈməʊ/
a children's rhyme used for choosing a person or thing. The child saying the rhyme points to each person or thing in turn on each beat of the rhyme and the person or thing pointed to on the last 'mo' is chosen. Different words are sometimes used instead of tigger and hollers. The words are:

The path descended the hill in a series of zigzags.
zigzag /ˈzɪɡzæɡ/
a line or pattern that looks like a series of letter W’s as it bends to the left and then to the right again

Utumnal colours.
autumnal /ɔːˈtʌmnəl/
​like or connected with autumn

He's in the autumn of his career.
autumn /ˈɔːtəm/
the season of the year between summer and winter, when leaves change colour and the weather becomes colder

The race ended with eight laps of a city centre circuit.
circuit /ˈsɜːrkɪt/
a line, route or journey around a place

An electrical circuit.
circuit /ˈsɜːrkɪt/
the complete path of wires and equipment along which an electric current flows

Ideally, this involves emptying out the refrigerator and unplugging it or turning it off at the circuit breaker.
circuit breaker /ˈsɜːrkɪt breɪkər/
​a device that will stop an electric current if it becomes dangerous

When the baby was born, I became the sole breadwinner.
breadwinner /ˈbredwɪnər/
a person who supports their family with the money they earn

I took a quick peek inside.
peek /piːk/
(SYNONYM peep) ​a quick and secret look at something that you should not be looking at

You'd to admire the slick presentation of last night’s awards ceremony.
slick /slɪk/
done or made in a way that is clever and efficient but often does not seem to be sincere or lacks important ideas

The belief that the mind's closely interrelated with the body.
interrelated /ˌɪntərɪˈleɪtɪd/
​closely connected and affecting each other

Tie the two ropes together with a knot.
knot /nɒt/
a join made by tying together two pieces or ends of string, rope, etc.

His clumsy fingers couldn't untie the knot.
clumsy /ˈklʌmzi/
​(of people and animals) moving or doing things in a way that is not smooth or steady or careful

I didn't wanna humiliate her in front of her colleagues.
humiliated /hjuːˈmɪlieɪtɪd/
to make somebody feel ashamed or stupid and lose the respect of other people

He was emphatic that he couldn't work with her.
emphatic /ɪmˈfætɪk/
(of a person) making it very clear what you mean by speaking with force

He was accused of plagiarizing his colleague's results.
plagiarize /ˈpleɪdʒəraɪz/
to copy another person’s ideas, words or work and pretend that they are your own

He's out on a spree.
spree /spriː/
​a short period of time that you spend doing one particular activity that you enjoy, but often too much of it

To go on a killing spree.
spree /spriː/
(used especially in newspapers) a period of activity, especially criminal activity

They stole thousands of pounds’ worth of goods in a two-day shoplifting spree.
shoplifting /ˈʃɑːplɪftɪŋ/
​the crime of stealing goods from a shop by deliberately leaving without paying for them
